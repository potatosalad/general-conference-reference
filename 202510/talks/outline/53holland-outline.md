### Summary

Elder Matthew S. Holland teaches that, like Jonah, we live in a fallen world and often falter, but if we refuse the “lying vanities,” turn to Christ, and honor our covenants (temple and service), we will receive His immediate, personalized mercy and find joy even amid trials.

### Haiku

Belly of the deep,  
I turn toward the temple—  
Mercy becomes mine.

### Key Points

1. A testimony of the Fall invites mercy, not excuses  
   Understanding that “all are fallen” helps us be patient with life’s brokenness and others’ imperfections without excusing sin; it softens criticism and nurtures Godlike compassion—“merciful, slow to anger, and of great kindness.”

2. Jonah points to Christ—the Sign of Jonas  
   Jonah’s three days in the deep foreshadow the Savior’s death and Resurrection; Christ’s Atonement offers immediate, tailored help—your “own mercy”—for every setback, sin, and sorrow.

3. Don’t forsake your own mercy: turn, covenant, and serve  
   Reject the adversary’s “lying vanities” by crying unto God, looking to the temple, clinging to covenants, and serving with sacrifice and thanksgiving—“pay that that [you] have vowed”—to experience God’s covenant love (hesed) and enduring joy.

### Scriptures and Gospel Principles

- Jonah 2:4–9 — “I will look again toward thy holy temple… They that observe lying vanities forsake their own mercy… I will pay that that I have vowed.”
- Jonah 4:2; Exodus 34:6 — God is “merciful, slow to anger, and of great kindness.”
- Matthew 12:39–41; Luke 11:29–32 — The “sign of Jonas” testifying of Christ’s Resurrection.
- Romans 3:23; Romans 5:12 — The universality and reality of the Fall.
- 2 Nephi 2:11, 25 — Opposition and the purpose of joy in mortality.
- 1 Nephi 1:20 — The Lord’s “tender mercies” to make us “mighty… unto… deliverance.”
- President Russell M. Nelson, “Joy and Spiritual Survival” (Oct. 2016) — Joy through focusing on Jesus Christ and the plan of salvation.
- Gospel principles: The Fall, the Atonement of Jesus Christ, repentance and covenants, temple worship, hesed (God’s loyal, covenantal mercy), gratitude and service as pathways to healing.

### Questions to Ponder

- Where do “lying vanities” show up in my life (habits, doubts, cynicism, distractions), and how can I replace them with faith-filled actions?
- What does it mean for Christ’s mercy to be “my own”—personally tailored to my specific wounds and weaknesses?
- When I feel “cast out of [His] sight,” how can turning to the temple and my covenants re-center my hope?
- In what ways could a stronger testimony of the Fall make me more patient—with myself, family members, and Church leaders?
- How can I “pay that that I have vowed” this week—through exactness in covenants, sacrificial service, or gratitude?
- What can I learn from Jonah’s resentment at Nineveh’s deliverance about rejoicing in God’s mercy for my “enemies”?
- When have I cried to the Lord “from the belly of hell,” and what helped me recognize His tender mercies?
- How can focusing on Jesus Christ (the Sign of Jonas) help me find joy regardless of what is happening—or not happening—in my life right now?
